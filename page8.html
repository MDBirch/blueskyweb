Legal Guidelines and Constraints that Impact Digital Projects


(1.7)


GDPR stands for General Data Protection Regulation
In May 2016 the EU unveiled the GDPR, a legal framework designed to harmonise and simplify disjointed data protection laws across European countries. GDPR was implemented in the UK on the 25th May 2018. GDPR aims to support the rights of consumers to know who holds data on them, to gain assurance they are using it for its original purpose and that it will be kept for no longer than necessary (reference). The Eu's Data protection Directive enacted in 1995 was outdated, as it was no longer relevant in today's digital age. It failed to address how data is stored, collected and transfered digitally. The regulations weren't able to keep up with pace of technological advancement (reference). GDPR is important because all businesses must adhere to the strict guidelines or they can be met with heavy fines. Data breaches must be reported within 72hrs, and how well their data response team is able to implement their plan to minimise any damage will affect how much a company is fined or penalised. Personal information that ends up in the wrong hands, can lead to financial damage and can also effect menatal health depending on what type of data is gathered, and how it is used against the consumer. GDPR is designed to make this kind of situation less common.
